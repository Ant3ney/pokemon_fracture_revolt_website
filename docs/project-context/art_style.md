Art Style Description

Pokémon Fracture / Pokémon Revolt has a stylized 3D civic-adventure look: polished, colorful, readable, and dramatic, with clean shapes and slightly idealized architecture. The world feels modern but not sci-fi. It uses real-world civic imagery — city halls, clock towers, plazas, train lines, scaffolding, banners, murals, signs, public gardens, stone streets, and mountain skylines — but pushes them through a charming, game-friendly 3D art direction.

The tone is political conflict through symbolism, not destruction. Instead of war zones, the game shows a region in transition: cracked pavement, divided banners, temporary barricades, construction cranes, posters, flags, and public squares filled with signs of reform. The land feels wounded, but not hopeless.

Mood

The mood is:

Tense, heroic, hopeful, colorful, and rebuilding.

It should not feel grimdark.
It should not feel cyberpunk.
It should not feel medieval.
It should not feel like a ruined apocalypse.

It should feel like:

“A beautiful region caught in a historic turning point.”

The conflict is serious, but the visual world still invites adventure.

Visual Pillars
1. Bright political drama

The game uses political imagery, but in a stylized, approachable way. Banners, emblems, posters, public murals, and civic architecture tell the story of the region’s factions.

The player should feel that the region is divided before anyone explains it.

2. Modern but timeless

The world should have trains, city streets, signage, street lamps, public plazas, construction zones, and modern civic buildings. But avoid extreme sci-fi elements like holograms, cyberpunk towers, neon megacities, giant screens everywhere, or glowing future-tech.

Think modern regional capital, not futuristic dystopia.

3. Hope after conflict

The art should show damage lightly and symbolically. Cracks, repairs, scaffolding, blocked roads, patched walls, and torn banners are enough.

The important visual message is not “everything is destroyed.”

The message is:

“This place is changing, and people are fighting over what it becomes next.”

4. Version color contrast

The two versions should share the same world, but feel emotionally different.

Fracture should lean into:

Cool blues
Silver
White stone
Purple/magenta fracture glow
Order, division, balance, repair
Cracks, split roads, and civic tension

Revolt should lean into:

Warm reds
Gold sunlight
Cream stone
Dark charcoal accents
Public movement, reform, voice, energy
Banners, signs, plazas, and uprising symbolism

Together, they create a strong dual identity:
Fracture = division and instability
Revolt = movement and change

Environment Style

The overworld should use stylized 3D architecture with strong readability.

Buildings should have simplified forms, clean silhouettes, and slightly exaggerated charm. Windows, roofs, signs, stairs, arches, railings, and streets should be readable from gameplay camera distance.

Good environment ingredients:

Civic plazas
Train stations
Modern town squares
Stone bridges
Public parks
Mountains in the distance
Government buildings
Construction zones
Street banners
Roadblocks
Repair scaffolding
Murals and posters
Faction-colored flags
Cracked but usable roads
Clean sunlight and strong shadows

Avoid:

Massive destruction
Burned-out war zones
Cyberpunk neon overload
Medieval castles as the main identity
Too much grime
Excessive realism
Horror lighting
Generic fantasy ruins
Lighting Style

Lighting should be one of the strongest parts of the game’s identity.

Use golden-hour and morning light often. The world should have dramatic shadows and strong contrast, but the sky should usually feel open and alive.

The lighting should say:

“The region is in trouble, but there is still a future.”

Best lighting traits:

Warm sun breaking through clouds
Long shadows across streets
Bright blue sky mixed with dramatic cloud banks
Soft volumetric rays
Clean rim light on buildings and banners
Slight glow on version symbols or cracks
Strong contrast without becoming dark or ugly
Material Style

Materials should be stylized but tactile.

Stone should look smooth and slightly painterly. Metal should be polished but not hyper-real. Cloth banners should have folds and texture. Roads should show cracks and repairs, but still feel clean enough for an adventure game.

Use:

Smooth stone
Painted signs
Soft fabric banners
Clean metal railings
Cobblestone and paved roads
Green plants and flowers
Warm window light
Light dust and wear

Do not overdo mud, ash, smoke, rust, or debris.

Character and Creature Compatibility

Even though the cover art has no creatures, the style should leave room for them.

That means the world cannot be too realistic or too dark. Creatures should be able to stand in the scene without looking childish or out of place.

So the environment should be:

Stylized enough for creature designs
Dramatic enough for the political story
Colorful enough for adventure
Clean enough for gameplay readability
One-Sentence Art Direction

A hopeful modern 3D creature-collector RPG set in a divided but beautiful region, using civic architecture, banners, construction, symbolic cracks, and golden sunlight to show political turmoil without turning the world into a dystopia.
